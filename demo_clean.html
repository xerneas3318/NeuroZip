Compression footprint - the table of last resort

bpp, compression ratio, and bytes-per-epoch for every codec. Buried on purpose, because reading these as the headline number is misleading (NeuroZip's bpp is higher than fidelity's - see the explainer below it).
codec bpp ratio bytes/epoch MSE
Why is NeuroZip's bpp higher than fidelity's at the same tier? Because NeuroZip's task loss forces the codec to preserve more information per epoch - specifically the CLIP-decodable structure that fidelity throws away. More information has a real Shannon cost in bits; you can't compress more information into the same number of bits. The bpp gap is the price of carrying task-relevant content; the retrieval lift up top is what you get for paying it.

A truly iso-rate comparison would binary-search NeuroZip's lambda_rate per tier until its bpp lands exactly on fidelity's. We didn't do that; the rate–retrieval plot above is the honest comparison instead (any horizontal line on it is an iso-rate slice). See ARCHITECTURE.md for the full information-theoretic framing and results.md for what an iso-rate table would look like (interpolated values; the gap is +4 to +9 pp at every tier).

Glossary - what every number on this page means

bpp
bits per sample. Average number of bits each raw EEG datapoint needs in the compressed representation. One epoch has 63 channels × 250 timesteps = 15,750 samples. bpp = total bits per epoch ÷ 15,750. Raw fp16 storage = 16 bpp. Lower is better (more compressed).
ratio
Compression ratio vs raw fp16: 16 ÷ bpp. A ratio of 144× means the compressed epoch is 1/144 the size of the original. Higher is better.
MSE
Mean squared error between the raw and decompressed EEG, computed on per-channel z-normalized signals (so units are "fraction of one channel's standard deviation, squared"). Pure waveform fidelity; doesn't care about semantic content. Lower is better.
top-1, top-5, top-10
Image-prompt retrieval accuracy. For each of the 200 test concepts, take the trial-averaged EEG, compress and decompress it, project to CLIP space, and rank all 200 CLIP image embeddings by cosine similarity. Top-k = fraction of concepts where the gold image is in the top k retrievals. Chance levels: 0.5% / 2.5% / 5.0% - anything above those beats random. The projector P (frozen judge) hits 18.5 / 45.0 / 65.0% on raw uncompressed EEG; that's the ceiling.
held-out
Independent classifier top-1. A separate EEG → concept classifier is trained on 60 of the 80 test reps per concept (averaging blocks of 10 reps for SNR) - it never sees codec output during its own training. At evaluation we score its top-1 on each codec's decompressed test EEG. This is the circularity defense: a high number means an independent judge can still identify the concept after compression, so the win isn't gaming the projector.
gold rank
Where the "correct" concept (highest CLIP-cos match to your query) was ranked in this codec's retrieval list. Rank 1 = top result; rank 200 = last. The lower the better.
CLIP cos
Cosine similarity between your free-text query (encoded by CLIP-text) and the nearest of the 200 known test concepts (encoded by CLIP-text). Tells you how on-vocabulary your query is. Cos ~0.7+ means the query is well- matched; lower means it's a stretch and retrieval will be noisier.
bits/symbol
Entropy of one quantized latent integer. Both codecs produce a fixed latent shape of 32×32 = 1024 symbols per epoch; what differs is the bits each symbol costs under the learned Laplace prior. bpp = bits/symbol × 1024 ÷ 15,750.
tier (low / med / high / xhigh)
Four rate-distortion operating points, controlled by lambda_rate in training. low = most aggressive compression (smallest bpp, biggest ratio); xhigh = least compression (biggest bpp, smallest ratio). NeuroZip uses slightly higher lambda_rate per tier to keep its bpp from drifting up under the task loss.
